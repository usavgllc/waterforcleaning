Buying vs. Building DI Water Systems

Analyzing the true costs of water purification for window cleaning professionals.

The Great Debate: DIY RO/DI vs. Purchasing Purity

For a growing window cleaning business, water is your most valuable raw material. As you transition to Water-Fed Pole (WFP) technology, the question inevitably arises: Should you build your own Reverse Osmosis/Deionization (RO/DI) trailer system, or should you simply buy high-purity deionized water from a professional supplier?

On the surface, making your own water seems like the ultimate cost-saving move. "Why pay for water when I can make it for pennies?" is the common refrain. However, when you dig into the startup costs, maintenance headaches, and hidden operational expenses, the math often tells a different story.

Startup Costs: The $2,000+ Hurdle

Building a reliable, professional-grade RO/DI system isn't as simple as connecting a few filters. To have a system that can produce high volumes of 0 TDS water efficiently, you're looking at a significant initial investment.

A basic entry-level portable RO/DI cart starts at $1,500. But for a true trailer-mounted or van-mounted setup that can handle full-day operations, you'll need:

  • High-Flow RO Membranes: $300 - $600 each.
  • Carbon and Sediment Pre-filters: $100 for a quality housing set.
  • Large DI Resin Vessels: $200 - $400.
  • Booster Pump (for low pressure areas): $300 - $500.
  • Holding Tank (100+ gallons): $200 - $400.
  • Fittings, Hoses, and Valves: $100 - $200.

By the time you've built a system that won't leave you stranded on a job site, you've easily spent $2,000 to $3,500 before you've cleaned your first window.

The Maintenance Headache

An RO/DI system is a living machine that requires constant attention. It isn't a "set it and forget it" solution. RO membranes are delicate and can be ruined by chlorine (if pre-filters aren't changed) or by sitting stagnant for too long (bacteria growth).

Regular maintenance costs include:

  • Pre-filter Changes: Every 2,000 - 5,000 gallons ($30 - $50).
  • DI Resin Replacement: Depending on your tap water hardness, this can be a frequent and expensive recurring cost.
  • Membrane Cleaning/Replacement: Every 1-3 years ($300+).
  • Winterization: If you live in a cold climate, the entire system must be drained or kept in a heated space to prevent catastrophic freeze damage.

Hidden Costs: Water Waste and Time

Perhaps the most overlooked factor in the "buy vs build" decision is water waste. Reverse Osmosis systems are inherently inefficient. For every 1 gallon of pure water produced, a typical RO system "rejects" (wastes) 2 to 4 gallons of water. If you are paying for your water at a shop or home, your utility bill will reflect this 4x consumption.

Then there is the "time cost." Making your own water takes time. Filling a 100-gallon tank can take hours depending on your flow rate. Maintaining the system, testing TDS at every stage, and troubleshooting leaks or pressure drops are hours spent working on your equipment rather than working for your customers.

The Break-Even Analysis

To determine if building a system is right for you, you must calculate your "break-even point."

If you purchase professional DI water at approximately $1.50 - $2.50 per gallon (depending on volume), and you use 20 gallons per day, your annual water cost is roughly $1,500 (based on 30 weeks of work). In this scenario, it would take you over two years just to pay off the startup cost of a $3,000 RO/DI trailer, without accounting for maintenance or your time.

For most residential-focused businesses or those just starting out, the capital is much better spent on marketing or better poles, while purchasing water provides a predictable, fixed expense with zero maintenance risk.

Who Should Build?

Building an RO/DI system makes sense for high-volume commercial crews who are cleaning large buildings 5-6 days a week, year-round. When you are consuming 100+ gallons of water per day, the economies of scale eventually tip in favor of on-site production, despite the maintenance burden.

Who Should Buy?

Buying professional DI water is the smarter choice for:

  • Owner-Operators: Your time is your most valuable asset. Don't waste it being a part-time water technician.
  • Startups: Keep your overhead low and your equipment simple.
  • Residential Specialists: Your water usage is likely low enough that a system will never truly pay for itself.
  • Quality-Obsessed Pros: Professional suppliers guarantee 0 TDS and ASTM-grade purity, which can be difficult to maintain consistently with a DIY mobile setup.

Conclusion: Focus on Your Business, Not Your Water

While the lure of "free" water is strong, the reality of building and maintaining an RO/DI system is a significant business commitment. For the majority of professional window cleaners, the reliability, convenience, and zero-maintenance nature of purchasing deionized water far outweigh the theoretical savings of a DIY system. Focus your energy on what you do best—cleaning windows and growing your brand—and leave the water chemistry to the experts.

Predictable Costs, Perfect Results

Skip the $3,000 trailer build and the maintenance headaches. Buy professional-grade, 0 TDS DI water and spend your time where it matters most: with your customers.

Buy vs. Build FAQs

How long do RO membranes actually last?

With proper care and high-quality pre-filtration, a membrane can last 2-3 years. However, high chlorine levels or "hard" source water can kill a membrane in months if not monitored daily.

Is 'DIY' DI water always 0 TDS?

Not necessarily. As the DI resin becomes exhausted, the TDS will begin to "creep" up. If you aren't monitoring your meter constantly, you may accidentally clean a house with 10-20 TDS water, resulting in spots and a costly re-clean.

What is the biggest 'hidden cost' of a trailer system?

Vehicle weight. A 100-gallon water tank weighs over 800 lbs. This increases fuel consumption, wear and tear on your vehicle's suspension and brakes, and may require a larger tow vehicle than you currently own.

Does DI water go 'bad' if I buy it in bulk?

As long as it is stored in a sealed, high-quality container away from direct sunlight, DI water will maintain its purity for many months. It does not "expire" in the traditional sense.
